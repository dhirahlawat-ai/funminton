Badminton Strategies

Master your game with proven techniques and tactical approaches

Singles Strategy

Court Coverage

In singles, players must cover the entire court alone. Maintain central base position and use split-step technique for efficient movement.

Attacking Backhand

Most players have weaker backhands. Exploit this by consistently targeting your opponent's backhand corner.

Four Corners Movement

Move your opponent to all four corners using long diagonals (up to 7.5 meters) to tire them out and create gaps.

High Serves

Use deep, high serves to push opponents to the back court, creating space for subsequent attacks.

Doubles Strategy

Formation Play

Master both offensive (front/back) and defensive (side by side) formations. Rotate seamlessly based on shuttle position.

Attack Conversion

Always try to keep the attack and convert opponent's attacks to defense. Keep shuttles low and focus on drives.

Placement Strategy

Place shuttles between opponents to cause confusion and indecision. Target the gap between front and back players.

Service Returns

Vary your serves between low and flick serves. Low serves are preferable as they're harder to attack.

Defensive Strategy

Ready Position

Keep low center of gravity, neutral grip, and racket head pointing down for explosive split steps.

Smash Defense

Focus on high clears or flat drives rather than half-court lifts. Use cross blocks to redirect smashes.

Quality Over Quantity

Make every defensive shot count. Turn defensive positions into counter-attacks when possible.

Patience

Outlast opponents through long rallies. Force them to make errors by consistently returning shots.

Badminton History

Journey through the evolution of badminton from ancient times to Olympic sport

Historical Timeline

Ancient Times (2000+ years ago)

Games like 'battledore and shuttlecock' played in China, India, and Greece using paddles to keep shuttlecock aloft.

1850s

British military officers in Pune, India adapt local game 'Poona' by adding a net to divide the court.

1873

Duke of Beaufort introduces the sport at Badminton House in Gloucestershire, England. Game gets its modern name.

1893

Badminton Association of England (BAE) formed, publishing first official set of rules.

1992

Badminton becomes official Olympic sport at Barcelona Games.

Badminton Rules

Complete guide to badminton rules, scoring, and court specifications

üèÜ Scoring System

Rally Point System (21 points)

  • Match consists of best of 3 games
  • First to 21 points wins a game
  • Point scored on every rally regardless of who serves
  • At 20-20, must win by 2 clear points
  • At 29-29, first to 30 points wins
  • Winner of game serves first in next game

üèüÔ∏è Court Dimensions

  • Length: 13.4m (44 feet) for both singles and doubles
  • Width: 6.1m (20 feet) for doubles, 5.18m (17 feet) for singles
  • Net height: 1.52m (5 feet) at center, 1.55m (5'1") at posts
  • Service courts: 6.5 feet from net to short service line

üéæ Service Rules

  • Serve must be hit below waist level (1.15m from ground)
  • Racket head must point downward when hitting shuttle
  • Serve in upward motion in one continuous movement
  • Both feet must remain stationary until serve is delivered
  • Serve diagonally to opponent's service court

‚ùå Common Faults

  • Shuttle lands outside court boundaries
  • Shuttle fails to pass over net
  • Player touches net with racket or body
  • Double hit of shuttle on same side
  • Service faults (too high, delayed, improper grip)

üè∏ Rackets

Weight Classification Guide

2U (90-94g)

Attacking Singles - Most power but less maneuverability

3U (85-89g)

Singles/Attacking Doubles - Great for aggressive play

4U (80-84g)

Optimal for most players - Good balance of speed and power

5U (75-79g)

Doubles/Defensive Singles - Faster swing speeds

Balance Types

Head Heavy

Power attacking, back court players. Increased smash and clear power.

Even Balance

All-round versatile style. Power at back, speed at front.

Head Light

Fast reactions, defensive shots, doubles play.
